# Style Mechanics

Use this file after the main rewrite. It refines grammar, register, punctuation, terminology, and mechanics. It must not override the target journal's author guidelines.

## Academic and Medical Register

- Prefer cautious, precise prose over conversational confidence.
- Avoid contractions, rhetorical questions, and promotional phrasing in manuscript prose.
- Use `we` only when the target journal and article type allow it; otherwise keep methods and results impersonal.
- Keep nominalisation useful, not excessive.
- Use inclusive medical language from `inclusive-medical-language.md`.
- Avoid `elderly`; prefer `older adults` unless quoting a source or naming a formal category.
- Avoid `suffer from` unless the participant's lived experience is directly being described; prefer `people with`, `patients with`, or `caregivers of people with`.

## Articles

Check articles carefully, especially for Chinese-influenced English:

- first mention of a singular count noun usually needs `a` or `an`;
- later mention of the same item usually needs `the`;
- generic plural nouns usually take no article;
- unique entities often take `the`;
- abstract nouns used generally often take no article.

Typical repair:

- weak: `The dementia causes burden for caregiver.`
- better: `Dementia can place substantial demands on caregivers.`

## Numbers, Units, and Statistics

- Use numerals for measurements, statistics, sample sizes, percentages, and scale scores unless the journal requires otherwise.
- Leave a space between values and units: `25 cm`, `3.2 s`.
- Keep statistical symbols and notation consistent.
- Report confidence intervals, effect sizes, and clinically meaningful values when available.
- Do not rewrite numerical values unless correcting an obvious typo identified by the user.
- Avoid using `significant` as a substitute for magnitude, direction, or uncertainty.

## Abbreviations and Terminology

- Define unavoidable abbreviations at first use in the abstract and main text separately if the journal requires this.
- Avoid abbreviations in titles unless they are universally recognized.
- Use one term consistently for the same concept.
- Check spelling of instruments, guidelines, databases, software, and reporting standards.
- For AI-related manuscripts, distinguish `AI chatbot`, `large language model`, `generative AI`, `rule-based chatbot`, `expert system`, and `digital platform` when the distinction matters.

## Sentence and Paragraph Checks

- Each sentence should express one main proposition.
- Dependent clauses must stay attached to a main clause.
- Do not join two independent clauses with only a comma.
- Split overloaded sentences rather than polishing them cosmetically.
- Each paragraph should have one controlling idea and supporting material.
- If a new idea appears, start a new paragraph.
- The last sentence of a paragraph is often the longest and weakest; check it explicitly.

## Transitions and Rhythm

- Prefer the smallest connective that does the job.
- Do not decorate every sentence with `Moreover`, `Furthermore`, `In addition`, `However`, or `Therefore`.
- Do not repeat the same transition in adjacent paragraphs.
- Avoid more than two nearby sentences with the same opening structure.
- Avoid overly symmetrical parallel sentences that sound artificial.

## Punctuation

- Avoid frequent em dashes. Prefer commas, parentheses, semicolons, or shorter sentences.
- Use semicolons only when both clauses are independent and closely related.
- Use colons sparingly when they create clear structure.
- Avoid slash-heavy expressions unless they are formal terms or journal style.

## Overclaim Checklist

Flag and soften:

- `prove`
- `conclusively`
- `unprecedented`
- `best`
- `superior`
- `first`
- `enhance`
- `improve`
- `promote`
- `prevent`
- `reduce`
- `increase`
- `impact`
- `influence`
- `determine`

Use the causality guardrail in `SKILL.md` to decide whether a stronger verb is justified.

## Integrity Rules

- Do not invent references, datasets, effect sizes, mechanisms, reviewer processes, ethics approvals, or registration details.
- Do not alter quantitative values unless the user asks for a correction and the evidence supports it.
- Do not upgrade association to causation.
- Do not imply broader generalisability than the study supports.
- Do not hide missing information behind polished vague language.
