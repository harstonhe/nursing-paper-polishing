# Inclusive Medical and Nursing Language

Apply these rules across all target journals unless a journal gives stricter wording. The goal is precise, respectful, non-stigmatizing language that matches contemporary medical and nursing publication norms.

## Age

- Prefer `older adults` or `older people` instead of `the elderly`.
- Avoid using age labels as nouns: write `older adults with frailty`, not `the frail elderly`.
- Use age ranges when available, such as `adults aged 65 years or older`.
- Avoid infantilizing or paternalistic language.

## Disability and Function

- Prefer person-first or identity-first language according to community preference and context.
- Use `people with disabilities` rather than `the disabled`.
- Avoid `suffers from`, `afflicted with`, `confined to a wheelchair`, and `wheelchair-bound`.
- Prefer `uses a wheelchair`, `has`, `lives with`, or precise functional descriptions.

## Disease, Symptoms, and Conditions

- Avoid defining people by a disease or symptom.
- Prefer `people with diabetes`, `patients with dementia`, `participants with urinary incontinence`, or `caregivers of people living with dementia`.
- Avoid stigmatizing phrasing such as `victim`, `case`, `noncompliant`, `dirty`, `clean`, `normal`, or `abnormal` when a more precise term exists.
- Replace `noncompliant` with `did not adhere to`, `reported difficulty following`, or `was unable to complete`, depending on evidence.

## Sex, Gender, and Reproductive Health

- Distinguish sex from gender when the study design or measures require it.
- Use `women`, `men`, `female participants`, `male participants`, or gender-diverse terms according to how data were collected.
- Avoid assuming gender from sex, pregnancy status, caregiving role, or occupation.
- Use inclusive reproductive language when appropriate, such as `pregnant people`, while preserving clarity if the study specifically enrolled women.

## Race, Ethnicity, Culture, and Country

- Report race, ethnicity, nationality, language, migration status, or culture only when relevant to the research question or analysis.
- Capitalize recognized racial and ethnic group names according to the journal style.
- Avoid treating countries, regions, or ethnic groups as proxies for biology, behavior, or culture.
- Avoid deficit framing. Describe structural, social, or health-system factors when these are the plausible explanation.

## Socioeconomic and Educational Status

- Prefer `people with low income`, `participants with lower educational attainment`, or precise measures.
- Avoid labels such as `poor people`, `low-class`, or `uneducated`.
- Distinguish individual-level socioeconomic indicators from area-level deprivation.

## Mental Health, Substance Use, and Stigma

- Prefer `people with mental health conditions`, `people with substance use disorders`, or precise diagnostic terms.
- Avoid `addict`, `abuser`, `crazy`, `mentally ill people`, and similar labels.
- Use `died by suicide`, not `committed suicide`.
- Avoid moralized language around care-seeking, adherence, or relapse.

## Nursing and Care Roles

- Use respectful terms for patients, family caregivers, nurses, nursing students, midwives, and care workers.
- Avoid implying that patients or caregivers are passive recipients unless the data support that framing.
- Do not overstate nurses' responsibility for outcomes that depend on staffing, policy, resources, or system-level constraints.

## AI, Digital Health, and Technology

- Avoid anthropomorphizing AI systems.
- Prefer `AI-based tool`, `chatbot`, `large language model`, `decision-support system`, or `digital intervention` as appropriate.
- Do not claim that AI `understands`, `knows`, `decides`, or `replaces clinicians` unless the manuscript is explicitly analyzing those claims.
- For non-causal evidence, write that tools `may support`, `may inform`, or `could be considered as part of`, rather than `improve care` or `enhance outcomes`.

## Common Replacements

- `elderly` -> `older adults` or `older people`
- `the aged` -> `older adults`
- `demented patients` -> `patients with dementia`
- `diabetics` -> `people with diabetes`
- `the disabled` -> `people with disabilities`
- `wheelchair-bound` -> `wheelchair users` or `people who use wheelchairs`
- `noncompliant patients` -> `patients who did not adhere to...` or `patients who reported barriers to...`
- `suffering from` -> `living with`, `with`, or `experiencing`
- `normal participants` -> `participants without [condition]`
- `males/females` as nouns -> `men/women`, `male/female participants`, or `participants assigned male/female at birth`, depending on measurement
