# Citation Positioning and Fairness to Prior Work

Use this file when revising Introduction, Background, Discussion, literature comparisons, novelty claims, or reviewer-facing justification.

## Core Principle

Citation is not only formatting. It tells the reader how the current manuscript stands relative to earlier work.

Use citations to clarify intellectual debt, evidence lineage, methodological borrowing, disagreement, and the precise gap addressed by the manuscript.

## Citation Functions

Classify each citation by its function:

- `support`: prior work supports a background premise, observed pattern, prevalence estimate, clinical concern, or theoretical claim;
- `borrow`: the manuscript adopts or adapts a method, reporting guideline, framework, measure, instrument, or analytic approach;
- `contrast`: the manuscript differs from prior work in population, setting, outcome, method, or interpretation;
- `context`: prior work explains why the topic matters for nursing, patients, caregivers, clinicians, organizations, education, or policy;
- `boundary`: prior work helps define what is known, what remains uncertain, and where generalization should stop;
- `critique`: prior work is being evaluated or challenged, which requires accurate and fair representation.

## Cite What Was Actually Read

Do:

- cite the primary source for its own data, methods, claims, or conclusions;
- cite a review for the review's synthesis, interpretation, or overall evidence map;
- cite guidelines, reporting standards, or tools for the exact procedure or checklist used;
- verify DOI, title, journal, and year when possible.

Do not:

- cite a source for a claim it did not make;
- cite a secondary source as if it were primary evidence when the primary source is available and important;
- copy a citation chain from another paper without reading the cited source;
- use abstracts, metadata pages, or inaccessible records as if they were full-text support for detailed claims.

## Fair Novelty and Gap Language

Novelty should be bounded by the actual literature search and article type.

Prefer:

- `Previous studies have examined ..., but evidence remains limited regarding ...`
- `Earlier work has established ..., whereas less is known about ...`
- `Although ... has been studied in ..., its application to ... remains unclear.`
- `To our knowledge, few studies have examined ... in [population/setting/design].`

Avoid unless verified:

- `This is the first study ...`
- `No studies have examined ...`
- `This topic has been ignored ...`
- `Prior research is inadequate ...`
- `The literature is completely lacking ...`

## Fairness Checks

Before polishing gap or Discussion paragraphs, ask:

- Have earlier studies been credited for what they already established?
- Is the gap specific enough to be defensible?
- Does the manuscript compare like with like, such as trial evidence with trial evidence or scoping evidence with scoping evidence?
- Are differences in population, culture, setting, health system, measurement, or technology version clearly stated?
- Is disagreement framed as a plausible difference rather than as proof that prior work was wrong?

## Reviewer-Safe Wording

Use cautious comparative language when evidence varies:

- `This finding is broadly consistent with ...`
- `This pattern extends previous work by ...`
- `The difference may reflect ...`
- `One possible explanation is ...`
- `These results should be interpreted alongside ...`

Do not overuse citation clusters. A single accurate citation is often better than a long list that blurs the evidence.
