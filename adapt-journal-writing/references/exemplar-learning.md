# Exemplar Article Learning

Use this reference to learn from public articles without copying them.

## Selection Criteria

Choose 2-3 exemplar articles that satisfy as many of these as possible:

- published in the target journal
- same article type or same review/method design
- topic-adjacent to the user's manuscript
- recent enough to reflect current journal practice
- full text or abstract is publicly accessible
- has clear DOI, URL, and publication year
- passes CrossRef or DOI metadata verification

When article type and topic conflict, article type usually matters more for writing structure.

## CrossRef Verification Gate

Verify the final 2-3 exemplar candidates before extracting writing patterns.

Use CrossRef or DOI metadata to confirm:

| Field | Acceptable evidence |
|---|---|
| DOI | DOI exists in CrossRef or official DOI metadata and resolves to a landing page |
| Title | Title substantially matches the candidate article |
| Journal | Container title, ISSN, or publisher page matches the target journal |
| Year | Publication year matches the article page, or differs only because of online-first vs issue publication |
| Access | Abstract, full text, or article landing page is accessible enough to inspect structure |

Classify each candidate:

| Status | Meaning | Use |
|---|---|---|
| `verified` | DOI/title/journal/year match and access is sufficient | Use as an exemplar |
| `partial` | Metadata exists but has a minor mismatch or limited access | Use cautiously and explain the limitation |
| `unverified` | No reliable DOI/CrossRef match, wrong journal, wrong title, or inaccessible source | Exclude and replace |

If a promising article is not indexed by CrossRef but is clearly available on the official journal site, treat it as `partial` only when the publisher page provides enough metadata to verify title, journal, date, and article type.

Do not learn writing patterns from `unverified` articles.

## Extraction Checklist

For each exemplar, record:

| Dimension | Questions |
|---|---|
| Verification | DOI, CrossRef status, journal match, year match, and access status |
| Abstract | Structured or narrative? What moves appear first and last? |
| Introduction | Does it open with burden, field gap, policy relevance, method gap, or conceptual problem? |
| Methods | How detailed are eligibility criteria, search strategy, analysis, and reporting checklist references? |
| Results | Are results organized by themes, evidence maps, study characteristics, outcomes, or chronological logic? |
| Discussion | Does it start with principal findings, implications, comparison with literature, limitations, or recommendations? |
| Claims | Are claims cautious, practice-oriented, mechanistic, policy-oriented, or methodological? |
| Headings | What headings/subheadings are common? |
| Tables/figures | Are evidence maps, flow diagrams, summary tables, or conceptual figures central? |

## Anti-Copying Rules

- Learn structure, not wording.
- Do not reproduce titles, topic sentences, or distinctive phrases.
- Do not mimic rhetorical flourishes unique to one article.
- Convert observations into abstract guidance such as "opens with public-health burden before narrowing to evidence gap."

## Synthesis Pattern

After reviewing exemplars, synthesize across them:

```text
Across the exemplar articles, the journal tends to:
- [pattern 1]
- [pattern 2]
- [pattern 3]

For this manuscript, that means:
- [adaptation 1]
- [adaptation 2]
- [adaptation 3]
```
