# Writing Adaptation Patterns

Use this reference after the target journal profile has been built.

## Scoping Review

Primary purpose: map a field, clarify concepts, summarize evidence distribution, identify gaps, and guide future research.

Prefer this logic:

1. field or practice problem
2. uncertainty or fragmented evidence
3. why a scoping review is appropriate
4. objectives framed as mapping questions
5. transparent search and selection methods
6. charting or synthesis approach
7. evidence landscape and themes
8. implications and knowledge gaps
9. limitations of search scope and included evidence

Avoid:

- causal claims
- efficacy claims
- clinical recommendations stronger than the evidence base
- "proves", "confirms", or "demonstrates" when the review only maps or summarizes

Useful claim verbs:

- `mapped`
- `identified`
- `summarized`
- `characterized`
- `highlighted`
- `revealed gaps in`
- `suggests priorities for`

## Systematic Review Or Meta-analysis

Emphasize protocol, eligibility, risk of bias, synthesis method, certainty of evidence, and effect estimates.

Do not use scoping-review language if the manuscript estimates effects or grades certainty.

## Original Research Article

Use the `nature-writing` evidence ladder:

`problem -> gap -> approach -> main evidence -> validation -> boundary -> implication`

## Methods Or Tool Paper

Use:

`bottleneck -> method principle -> implementation -> benchmark -> ablation or validation -> reuse boundary`

## Adaptation Checks

Before finalizing any rewrite, check:

- Does the article type match the target journal category?
- Does the abstract follow the journal's required structure?
- Are claims bounded by the study design?
- Are required reporting items visible?
- Does the section order match official guidance or strong exemplar patterns?
- Are missing author facts marked rather than invented?
